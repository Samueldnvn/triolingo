The Age of Reason and Linear Tactics

18th-Century Warfare and Limited Wars

Introduction

Eighteenth-century warfare was characterized by linear formations of infantry firing disciplined volleys, careful maneuver, limited war objectives, and the professionalization of armies. Wars were fought for specific territorial or dynastic objectives rather than total annihilation. Frederick the Great's Prussia pioneered new military methods that made Prussia a major power.

LINEAR FORMATIONS

Two-Rank and Three-Rank Systems

By the 18th century, the phalanx and deep columns were obsolete. Infantry deployed in lines two or three ranks deep, maximizing controlled firepower. The first rank fired, then stepped back to reload while second rank fired, creating continuous rolling volleys. This delivered devastating fire that could shatter attacking formations.

Firepower as the Deciding Factor

Unlike earlier eras where shock (physical collision of masses) was decisive, firearms made firepower decisive. The side that could deliver more accurate and sustained volleys typically prevailed. This changed battlefield dynamics significantly.

FREDERICK THE GREAT

Prussian Military Innovator

Frederick II of Prussia (1740-1786) developed innovative tactics that allowed Prussia to defeat larger opponents. His key contribution was the "oblique order"—attacking an enemy flank with concentrated forces while refusing (holding back) his opposite flank. This allowed Frederick's numerically inferior armies to defeat larger enemies at a decisive point while avoiding engagement where weak.

Battle of Rossbach (1757)
Frederick defeated a combined French and Austrian army twice his size through rapid movement, seizing the initiative, and attacking from an unexpected position. This battle demonstrated Prussian skill in maneuver and surprise.

SEVEN YEARS WAR (1756-1763)

First Global Conflict

The Seven Years War was fought in Europe, North America, India, and at sea—a global conflict. Frederick's Prussia faced nearly all of Europe but survived through battlefield brilliance and the failure of enemies to coordinate effectively. In North America, British and American colonial forces captured French territories. The war established Prussia as a major power and Britain as a naval power while setting conditions for the American Revolution.
