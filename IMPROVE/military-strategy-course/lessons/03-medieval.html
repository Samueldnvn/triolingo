Medieval Warfare

The Age of Castles, Chivalry, and Emerging Professional Armies

Introduction

Medieval warfare (approximately 500-1500 CE) represents a transitional period between the ancient world and early modern military systems. Characterized by feudal military obligations, castle fortifications, and the dominance of armored cavalry, medieval warfare evolved significantly over its millennium-long span. By the late medieval period, professional infantry and gunpowder weapons began challenging the ascendancy of knights and castles, setting the stage for the military revolution of the early modern period.

FEUDAL MILITARY ORGANIZATION

The Land-Based Military System

The feudal system was fundamentally a military arrangement. Kings granted land (fiefs) to nobles in exchange for military service. This service typically amounted to 40 days per year during the early medieval period, after which the noble could return home unless voluntary obligation extended. This system created armies dominated by a relatively small warrior aristocracy—the knights—who owed service as heavy cavalry.

Advantages and Limitations of Feudal Forces

Feudal Military Characteristics

Advantages: Created a loyal military class bound by personal obligations to lords. Knights were heavily armored and trained from childhood. The system required no direct payment from the king (in the form of salaries), making it economically simpler for medieval monarchs.

Limitations: Service was limited in duration (40 days typical). Feudal armies could not sustain long campaigns far from home. Raising large forces required summoning nobles, who in turn summoned their own vassals, making mobilization slow and coordination complex. Kings had limited direct command over individual knights beyond what their personal relationships and authority permitted.

Evolution Paid Standing Forces

As medieval kingdoms grew more administratively sophisticated and wealthier, monarchs increasingly hired mercenaries and raised paid troops. These professional soldiers (often paid scutage—shield money—by nobles who preferred not to serve) provided kings with armies that could campaign year-round and whose loyalty was to the king rather than to intermediate lords. This development toward professionally-paid armies was gradual but significant.

CASTLES AND SIEGE WARFARE

Fortification and Persistence

Castles were central to medieval warfare. They provided bases for controlling territory, secure storage for wealth and supplies, and strongpoints from which to launch or resist attacks. Medieval warfare often focused less on decisive battles and more on controlling castles, which dictated control of the countryside. A castle could be held by a relatively small garrison against much larger forces, making them extremely cost-effective fortifications.

Castle Design Evolution

Period Design Type Characteristics
Early (11th-12th c.) Motte and Bailey Earth mound (motte) with wooden tower, bailey courtyard, palisade
High Medieval (12th-13th c.) Stone Castles Stone walls, towers, gatehouses, keeps, curtain walls
Late Medieval (13th-15th c.) Concentric Castles Multiple curtain walls, concentric rings of defense

Siege Methods

Siege warfare against castles took several approaches, each with advantages and trade-offs:

  • Blockade/Starvation: The most common and reliable method. Surround the castle, cut off food and water supplies, and wait for defenders to surrender. This could take months or longer but typically succeeded if maintained long enough.
  • Direct Assault: Storming walls using siege towers, ladders, or artillery. High risk of casualties, but faster if successful. Used when urgency or other considerations made blockade impossible.
  • Undermining: Digging tunnels beneath walls to collapse them. Required skilled miners and time but could bring down otherwise impregnable fortifications.
  • Treachery: Getting defenders to open gates, often through bribery or internal supporters. Could succeed where military force would fail but required opportunity and planning.
The Defensive Advantage

Throughout medieval warfare, fortification generally favored the defender. Roughly, a force needed 10:1 superiority to successfully assault a well-defended castle. This meant that medieval campaigns focused on taking castles more than seeking major field battles. Commanders avoided attacking strong castles unless necessary, preferring to bypass or blockade them.

KNIGHTS, CAVALRY, AND INFANTRY EVOLUTION

The Changing Battlefields

Throughout most of the medieval period, the armored knight on horseback was the dominant battlefield figure. Knights were heavily armored men-at-arms, protected by chainmail or later plate armor, who fought from horseback with lance and sword. The momentum of a cavalry charge combined with the protection of armor made cavalry nearly unstoppable in frontal attacks against infantry—or so it seemed for centuries.

Knightly Equipment

Item Purpose
Mail Armor Interlocked metal rings protecting entire body
Plate Armor (later) Bolted metal plates over mail for superior protection
Saddle High cantles for rider security in combat
Lance For delivering the initial charge impact
Sword For melee combat after lance broken
Warhorse Large trained horse bred for battlefield use

Challenges to Cavalry Dominance

By the late medieval period, several developments challenged cavalry dominance:

The Changing Battlefield
Missile Weapons: Longbows and crossbows could kill knights at range, penetrating armor at short distances. The English longbow could fire 10+ arrows per minute in volleys, devastating charging cavalry before impact.

Professional Infantry: Swiss pikemen and later German landsknechts formed deep pike formations that braced against cavalry charges. Pikes could stop charging knights before they contacted, with infantry then closing against dismounted knights.

Terrain: Skilled generals chose battlefield terrain unfavorable to cavalry—rough ground, obstacles that broke formations—neutralizing the cavalry advantage.

Key Battles Demonstrating Infantry Power

Battle of Courtrai (1302)

Flemish infantry defeated French knights in a battle known as the Battle of the Golden Spurs. This demonstrated that infantry could defeat heavy cavalry under favorable conditions, especially when supported by good terrain and defensive positions.

Battle of Bannockburn (1314)

Scottish spearmen under Robert the Bruce defeated Edward II's English cavalry, showing that disciplined infantry with good ground and proper spearmanship could overcome mounted knights.

Battle of St. Jakob an der Birs (1444)

Though the Swiss were ultimately defeated by a larger French force, their performance against superior numbers demonstrated the growing effectiveness of professional infantry formations.
