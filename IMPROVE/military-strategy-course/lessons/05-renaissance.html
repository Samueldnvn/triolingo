Renaissance Warfare

Military Innovation in the Age of Discovery

Introduction

Renaissance warfare (c. 14th-early 17th century) saw the institutionalization of gunpowder weapons, the professionalization of armies, and the development of sophisticated military theory. Armies grew larger and more expensive, states increasingly took direct control of military power, and naval warfare underwent profound changes. This era also witnessed the beginning of colonial and global warfare as European powers expanded overseas.

PIKE AND SHOT TACTICS

Combined Arms for Gunpowder Era

Pike and shot formations built on the Spanish tercio but evolved more flexible arrangements. Pikemen formed the front to protect musketeers, while musketeers fired from behind or the flanks, providing firepower. By the early 17th century, many European armies moved toward shallower formations with better flexibility than the deep tercio.

Swiss Pikemen
Swiss pikemen formed deep columns and defeated cavalry at battles like Nancy (1477) and Marignano (1515). They demonstrated professional infantry's ability to dominate battlefields, though firearms gradually increased in importance.

MACHIAVELLI ON WAR

Art of War (1521)

Niccolo Machiavelli, famous for "The Prince," also wrote on military affairs. In "Art of War," Machiavelli argued that citizen armies were superior to mercenaries and that Florence should maintain a militia for its defense. He criticized reliance on condottieri (mercenary captains), whose loyalty was questionable. Machiavelli advocated citizen-soldiers motivated by patriotism and properly trained by the state.

THIRTY YEARS WAR (1618-1648)

Religious War and Military Innovation

The Thirty Years War was a devastating religious conflict involving most European powers. It demonstrated massed field artillery, continued evolution of pike and shot tactics, and the increasing size of armies. Gustavus Adolphus of Sweden contributed mobile light artillery and improved combined arms coordination. The war caused massive civilian casualties in central Europe and demonstrated how warfare could devastate society.
