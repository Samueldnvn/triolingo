3D Vectors

The Building Blocks of 3D Space

🔨 ENTERING 3D SPACE 🔨

Vectors with three components describe direction and magnitude in 3D space. Essential for physics, engineering, and graphics!

Definition: 3D Vector

A vector in 3D is written with three components:

$$\vec{v} = \langle a, b, c \rangle = \begin{bmatrix}a \\ b \\ c\end{bmatrix}$$

Example: 3D Vectors

Position vector of point (3,4,5): $$\vec{r} = \langle 3, 4, 5 \rangle$$

Velocity vector moving at speed 5 in direction (2,1,2): $$\vec{v} = \frac{5}{3}\langle 2, 1, 2 \rangle$$

Magnitude and Unit Vectors

Magnitude of 3D Vector

$$|\vec{v}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}$$

Example: Magnitude

Find $$|\langle 1, 2, 2 \rangle|$$

$$|\langle 1, 2, 2 \rangle| = \sqrt{1 + 4 + 4} = \sqrt{9} = 3$$

Unit Vector

A unit vector has magnitude 1 and points in a specific direction:

$$\hat{u} = \frac{\vec{u}}{|\vec{u}|}$$

📋 STANDARD UNIT VECTORS

  • $$\vec{i} = \langle 1, 0, 0 \rangle$$ - points along x-axis
  • $$\vec{j} = \langle 0, 1, 0 \rangle$$ - points along y-axis
  • $$\vec{k} = \langle 0, 0, 1 \rangle$$ - points along z-axis
  • Any 3D vector: $$\vec{v} = a\vec{i} + b\vec{j} + c\vec{k}$$

Vector Operations

Addition, Scalar Multiplication, and Products

Basic Operations

Vector Addition

$$\vec{v} + \vec{w} = \langle a_1+a_2, b_1+b_2, c_1+c_2 \rangle$$

Scalar Multiplication

$$k\vec{v} = k\langle a, b, c \rangle = \langle ka, kb, kc \rangle$$

Example

$$2\langle 1, 3, 5 \rangle + \langle 4, 0, 2 \rangle = \langle 6, 6, 12 \rangle$$

The Dot Product

Definition: Dot Product

$$\vec{v} \cdot \vec{w} = v_1 w_1 + v_2 w_2 + v_3 w_3$$

The result is a SCALAR (number), not a vector!

Example: Dot Product

$$\langle 1, 2, 3 \rangle \cdot \langle 4, 5, 6 \rangle = 4 + 10 + 18 = 32$$

Geometric Meaning

The dot product relates to the angle θ between vectors:

$$\vec{v} \cdot \vec{w} = |\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\cos(\theta)$$

This means: Perpendicular vectors have dot product = 0

The Cross Product

🛠️ THE CROSS PRODUCT IS SPECIAL TO 3D!

Definition: Cross Product

$$\vec{v} \times \vec{w} = \begin{vmatrix}\vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k} \\ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ w_1 & w_2 & w_3\end{vmatrix}$$

The result is a VECTOR perpendicular to both input vectors!

Standard Cross Products

  • $$\vec{i} \times \vec{j} = \vec{k}$$ (right-hand rule)
  • $$\vec{j} \times \vec{k} = \vec{i}$$
  • $$\vec{k} \times \vec{i} = \vec{j}$$
  • $$\vec{j} \times \vec{i} = -\vec{k}$$ (order matters!)
⚠️ REMEMBER: Cross product = ANTI-COMMUTATIVE! $$\vec{v} \times \vec{w} \neq \vec{w} \times \vec{v}$$

Lines and Planes in 3D

Describing Paths and Surfaces

Lines in 3D Space

Parametric Equations of a Line

$$x = x_0 + at, \quad y = y_0 + bt, \quad z = z_0 + ct$$

OR in vector form:

$$\vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_0 + t\vec{v}$$

where $$\vec{r}_0 = \langle x_0, y_0, z_0 \rangle$$ is a point and $$\vec{v} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$$ is direction

Example: Line through (1,2,3) with direction (2,-1,4)

$$\vec{r}(t) = \langle 1, 2, 3 \rangle + t\langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle$$
$$x = 1 + 2t, \quad y = 2 - t, \quad z = 3 + 4t$$

Planes in 3D Space

Equation of a Plane

Given a point $$(x_0, y_0, z_0)$$ and normal vector $$\vec{n} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$$:

$$a(x-x_0) + b(y-y_0) + c(z-z_0) = 0$$

Simplified form:

$$ax + by + cz = d$$

Example: Plane through (1,2,3) with normal (2,-1,4)

$$2(x-1) - (y-2) + 4(z-3) = 0$$
$$2x - y + 4z - 12 = 0$$

📋 KEY RELATIONSHIPS

  • Line direction vector ⟂ Plane normal vector: PARALLEL to plane
  • Line direction vector ∥ Plane normal vector: PERPENDICULAR to plane
  • Two planes are parallel if normals are parallel
  • Two planes intersect in a line
