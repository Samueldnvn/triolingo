Line Integrals, Surface Integrals & Theorems | Calculus III

Overview
Calculus on Curves and Surfaces

Vector calculus extends integration to curved paths and surfaces. Contains beautiful theorems connecting integrals over regions to boundaries.

Vector Fields
Definition
Vector Field
2D Field
$$\vec{F}(x,y) = \langle P(x,y), Q(x,y) \rangle = P\vec{i} + Q\vec{j}$$
3D Field
$$\vec{F}(x,y,z) = \langle P, Q, R \rangle$$
Interpretation

Assigns a vector to each point in space: velocity field, force field, electromagnetic field

Example
Common Vector Fields
Radial Field

$$\vec{F} = r\vec{r} = \langle x, y \rangle$$ (points outward)

Rotation Field

$$\vec{F} = \langle -y, x \rangle$$ (rotates counterclockwise)

Gravitational Field

$$\vec{F} = -\frac{GMm}{|r|^3}\vec{r}$$ (points toward origin)

Line Integrals of Vector Fields (Work)
Definition
Line Integral
Formula
$$\int_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \int_a^b \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \cdot \vec{r}'(t) dt$$
Physical Meaning

Represents work done by field $$\vec{F}$$ moving particle along curve C

Example
Computing Work

$$\int_C \langle y, x \rangle \cdot d\vec{r}$$ along C: $$(0,0) \to (2,0) \to (2,3)$$

C1: $$\vec{r}_1 = \langle t, 0 \rangle, 0\leq t\leq 2$$
$$\int_0^2 \langle 0, t \rangle \cdot \langle 1, 0 \rangle dt = 0$$

C2: $$\vec{r}_2 = \langle 2, t \rangle, 0\leq t\leq 3$$
$$\int_0^3 \langle t, 2 \rangle \cdot \langle 0, 1 \rangle dt = \int_0^3 2 dt = 6$$

$$W = 0 + 6 = 6$$

Fundamental Theorem of Line Integrals
Theorem
Conservative Fields
Theorem
If $$\vec{F} = \nabla f$$ (conservative), then $$\int_C \nabla f \cdot d\vec{r} = f(\vec{r}(b)) - f(\vec{r}(a))$$
Path Independence

For conservative fields, line integral depends only on endpoints, not path!

Test for Conservative Field
2D
$$\vec{F} = \langle P, Q \rangle$$ is conservative if $$\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}$$
3D
$$\vec{F} = \langle P, Q, R \rangle$$ conserv. if $$\nabla \times \vec{F} = \vec{0}$$
Surface Integrals
Definition
Surface Integral
Formula
$$\iint_S \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{S} = \iint_D \vec{F}(\vec{r}(u,v)) \cdot (\vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_v) du dv$$
Flux

Measures how much "fluid" passes through surface S. Represents flux of vector field through surface

Example
Flux Through Rectangle

$$\vec{F} = \langle 0,0,1 \rangle$$ through unit square in xy-plane

$$d\vec{S} = \langle 0,0,1 \rangle dx dy$$ (normal upward)

$$\Phi = \iint \langle 0,0,1 \rangle \cdot \langle 0,0,1 \rangle dx dy = \iint 1 dx dy = 1$$

Stokes' Theorem
Theorem
Stokes' Theorem
Formula
$$\oint_C \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \vec{F}) \cdot d\vec{S}$$
Meaning

Line integral around boundary S equals surface integral of curl over S

Curl
Formula
$$\nabla \times \vec{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k} \\ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z \\ P & Q & R \end{vmatrix} = \langle R_y - Q_z, P_z - R_x, Q_x - P_y \rangle$$
Divergence Theorem
Theorem
Divergence Theorem (Gauss)
Formula
$$\iint_S \vec{F} \cdot d\vec{S} = \iiint_E (\nabla \cdot \vec{F}) dV$$
Meaning

Flux through surface equals triple integral of divergence over volume

Divergence
Formula
$$\nabla \cdot \vec{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}$$
