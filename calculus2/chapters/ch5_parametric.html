Chapter 5: Parametric Equations and Polar Coordinates

📋 Chapter Overview

So far, we've worked with functions y = f(x) where y is expressed explicitly in terms of x. Parametric equations allow both x and y to be functions of a third parameter. Polar coordinates offer an alternative way to describe points in the plane using distance and angle rather than rectangular coordinates.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand parametric equations and their graphs
  • Convert between parametric and rectangular forms
  • Compute derivatives with respect to x for parametric curves
  • Calculate arc length and surface area using parameters
  • Convert between rectangular and polar coordinates
  • Graph polar equations and identify symmetry
  • Compute derivatives, arc length, and areas in polar form

🌍 Real-World Applications

  • Physics: Projectile motion, orbital mechanics
  • Engineering: CAM systems, robotics
  • Computer Graphics: Bézier curves, animation paths
  • Navigation: Radar, sonar, GPS systems

Lesson 32: Parametric Equations

📖 Introduction

Instead of writing y = f(x), we can write two equations: x = f(t) and y = g(t), where t is called the parameter. This gives us more flexibility to describe curves that wouldn't pass the vertical line test!

📚 Definition of Parametric Equations

A parametric curve consists of two functions:

$$x = f(t), \quad y = g(t)$$

where t is in some interval I.

Key Concepts:

  • Parameter t: The independent variable
  • Direction of motion: As t increases, the point (x(t), y(t)) moves along the curve
  • Eliminating the parameter: Solve for t and substitute to get equation in x and y

📚 Common Parametric Curves

Circle (radius r):

$x = r \cos t, \quad y = r \sin t, \quad 0 \leq t \leq 2\pi$

Line through (x₀, y₀) with slope m:

$x = x_0 + t, \quad y = y_0 + mt, \quad -\infty < t < \infty$

Cycloid (point on rolling wheel):

$x = r(t - \sin t), \quad y = r(1 - \cos t)$

🔢 Example 1: Eliminate parameter from x = t², y = t³

Step 1: From x = t², we get t = ±√x (but we have to be careful)
Step 2: For the curve, we can use t³ = t · t²
Step 3: Since y = t³ and x = t², we have: t = y/t² = y/x (when x ≠ 0)
Step 4: Substitute: y = (y/x) · x = y ✓ (checks out)
Step 5: Better approach: $y^2 = t^6 = (t^2)^3 = x^3$, so $y^2 = x^3$ or $\\pm y = x^{3/2}$
Step 6: Final equation: $y^2 = x^3$ (a cusp curve)

Lesson 33: Calculus with Parametric Equations

📖 Introduction

How do we find the slope of a parametric curve? We can't just find dy/dx directly, but we can use the chain rule to express dy/dx in terms of the derivatives with respect to the parameter!

📚 First Derivative (Slope)

If x = f(t) and y = g(t), then:

$$\\frac{dy}{dx} = \\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \\frac{g'(t)}{f'(t)}, \\quad \\text{provided } f'(t) \\neq 0$$

Key Points:

  • Both derivatives are with respect to t (the parameter)
  • The result is in terms of t, not x or y
  • Watch out for horizontal tangent lines (dy/dt = 0) and vertical tangent lines (dx/dt = 0)

📚 Second Derivative

$$\\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \\frac{d}{dx}\\left(\\frac{dy}{dx}\\right) = \\frac{d/dt(dy/dx)}{dx/dt}$$

IMPORTANT: Don't use quotient rule! Differentiate dy/dx with respect to t, then divide by dx/dt.

🔢 Example: Find dy/dx for x = t² - 2t, y = t³ - 3t

Step 1: Find dx/dt = 2t - 2 = 2(t - 1)
Step 2: Find dy/dt = 3t² - 3 = 3(t² - 1) = 3(t - 1)(t + 1)
Step 3: Use formula: $\\frac{dy}{dx} = \\frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \\frac{3(t-1)(t+1)}{2(t-1)}$
Step 4: Simplify (cancelling t-1): $\\frac{dy}{dx} = \\frac{3(t+1)}{2}$
Step 5: Note: t = 1 gives dx/dt = dy/dt = 0, so the derivative formula doesn't apply there

Lesson 34: Arc Length and Surface Area with Parametrics

📖 Introduction

The arc length formula from Chapter 1 can be transformed for parametric curves. This is particularly useful for calculating the length of curves that are easier to describe parametrically.

📚 Arc Length Formula (Parametric)

If x = f(t), y = g(t) with smooth derivatives, and the curve is traced once as t goes from α to β:

$$L = \\int_{\\alpha}^{\\beta} \\sqrt{\\left(\\frac{dx}{dt}\\right)^2 + \\left(\\frac{dy}{dt}\\right)^2} \\, dt$$

📚 Surface Area (Rotation about x-axis)

$$S = 2\pi \\int_{\\alpha}^{\\beta} y \\sqrt{\\left(\\frac{dx}{dt}\\right)^2 + \\left(\\frac{dy}{dt}\\right)^2} \\, dt$$

This is the same structure as the formula for y = f(x), but expressed in terms of t and both dx/dt and dy/dt.

🔢 Example: Find length of x = cos t, y = sin t from t=0 to t=2π

Step 1: dx/dt = -sin t, dy/dt = cos t
Step 2: Use arc length formula: $L = \int_0^{2\\pi} \\sqrt{(-\\sin t)^2 + (\\cos t)^2} \\, dt$
Step 3: Simplify: $\int_0^{2\\pi} \\sqrt{\sin^2 t + \\cos^2 t} \\, dt = \int_0^{2\\pi} \\sqrt{1} \\, dt$
Step 4: Integrate: $\\int_0^{2\\pi} 1 \\, dt = [t]_0^{2\\pi} = 2\\pi - 0 = 2\\pi$
Step 5: Answer: 2π (circumference of unit circle, as expected!)

Lesson 35: Polar Coordinates

📖 Introduction

Polar coordinates describe points in the plane using (r, θ) where r is the distance from the origin and θ is the angle from the positive x-axis. This coordinate system is natural for problems with circular or radial symmetry.

📚 Polar Coordinates

A point has polar coordinates $(r, \\theta)$ where:

• $r$ = distance from the origin
• $\\theta$ = angle measured from the positive x-axis

The point is located: ($r \\cos \\theta$, $r \\sin \\theta$)

📚 Converting Between Coordinate Systems

Polar to Rectangular:

$$x = r \\cos \\theta, \\quad y = r \\sin \\theta, \\quad x^2 + y^2 = r^2, \\quad \\tan \\theta = \\frac{y}{x}$$

Rectangular to Polar:

$$r = \\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \\quad \\theta = \\arctan\\left(\\frac{y}{x}\\right)$$

Important: When finding θ, consider which quadrant the point is in to get the correct angle!

🔢 Example 1: Convert polar point (2, π/3) to rectangular

Step 1: x = r cos θ = 2 \\cdot cos(\\pi/3) = 2 \\cdot \\frac{1}{2} = 1
Step 2: y = r sin θ = 2 \\cdot sin(\\pi/3) = 2 \\cdot \\frac{\\sqrt{3}}{2} = \\sqrt{3}
Step 3: Answer: (1, √3)

Lesson 36: Polar Graphs and Symmetry

📖 Introduction

Polar graphs can create beautiful, complex shapes with simple equations. Understanding symmetry helps us sketch these graphs efficiently.

📚 Common Polar Curves

Circles:

  • $r = a$ (circle centered at origin, radius a)
  • $r = 2a \cos \theta$ (circle to the right of origin)
  • $r = 2a \sin \theta$ (circle above origin)

Lines:

  • $\theta = \text{constant}$ (ray from origin)
  • $r = \frac{a}{\cos \theta}$ or $r = \frac{a}{\sin \theta}$ (vertical/horizontal lines)

Roses (petals):

$r = a \cos(k\\theta)$ or $r = a \sin(k\\theta)$
• If k is odd: k petals
• If k is even: 2k petals

Limaçons:

$r = a \\pm b \\cos \\theta$ or $r = a \\pm b \\sin \\theta$

Spirals:

$r = a\\theta$ (Archimedean spiral)

📚 Symmetry Tests

  • 关于 x-axis: Replace θ by -θ. If equation unchanged, symmetric about x-axis
  • 关于 y-axis: Replace θ by π-θ. If equation unchanged, symmetric about y-axis
  • 关于 origin: Replace (r, θ) by (-r, θ) or (r, θ+π). If same, symmetric about origin

Lesson 37: Calculus in Polar Coordinates

📖 Introduction

We can do calculus in polar coordinates too! The key is to remember how to convert back and forth between polar and rectangular forms when needed.

📚 Tangent Lines in Polar

For a polar curve r = f(θ), use:

$$\\frac{dy}{dx} = \\frac{dy/d\\theta}{dx/d\\theta} = \\frac{\\frac{d}{d\\theta}(r \\sin \\theta)}{\\frac{d}{d\\theta}(r \\cos \\theta)}$$

Use product rule on both numerator and denominator!

📚 Arc Length in Polar

$$L = \\int_{\\alpha}^{\\beta} \\sqrt{r^2 + \\left(\\frac{dr}{d\\theta}\\right)^2} \\, d\\theta$$

Derivation:

Starting from parametric with x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ, we get the formula above.

🔢 Example: Find the length of r = 1 + cos θ from θ=0 to θ=2π

Step 1: Given r = 1 + cos θ, find dr/dθ = -sin θ
Step 2: Use arc length formula with both terms squared
Step 3: Integrand = $\sqrt{(1 + \\cos \\theta)^2 + (-\\sin \\theta)^2}$
Step 4: Recognize this equals $\sqrt{2 + 2\\cos \\theta}$ after expanding
Step 5: Using half-angle identity, length = 8 (for cardioid)

Lesson 38: Area in Polar Coordinates

📖 Introduction

Finding area in polar coordinates uses integration, but the formula looks different because we're working with wedge-shaped sectors rather than rectangles.

📚 Area Formula

The area enclosed by r = f(θ) where α ≤ θ ≤ β is:

$$A = \\frac{1}{2} \\int_{\\alpha}^{\\beta} [f(\\theta)]^2 \\, d\\theta = \\frac{1}{2} \\int_{\\alpha}^{\\beta} r^2 \\, d\\theta$$

Key Points:

  • The $\frac{1}{2}$ comes from the sector area formula $\frac{1}{2}r^2θ$
  • We integrate r², not just r
  • The limits α and β are key - they're usually given or found from intersections

📚 Area Between Two Curves

$$A = \\frac{1}{2} \\int_{\\alpha}^{\\beta} [r_{outer}^2 - r_{inner}^2] \\, d\\theta$$

Always subtract inner from outer (larger r minus smaller r)!

🔢 Example 1: Find area of circle r = 2a cos θ

Step 1: The circle is traced as θ goes from -π/2 to π/2
Step 2: Area = $\frac{1}{2} \int_{-\\pi/2}^{\\pi/2} (2a \\cos \\theta)^2 \, d\\theta$
Step 3: Simplify: $\frac{1}{2} \int_{-\\pi/2}^{\\pi/2} 4a^2 \\cos^2 \\theta \, d\\theta = 2a^2 \int_{-\\pi/2}^{\\pi/2} \\cos^2 \\theta \, d\\theta$
Step 4: Use half-angle: $\cos^2 \\theta = \frac{1 + \\cos 2\\theta}{2}$
Step 5: Area = $2a^2 \int_{-\\pi/2}^{\\pi/2} \\frac{1 + \\cos 2\\theta}{2} \, d\\theta = a^2[\\theta + \frac{\sin 2\\theta}{2}]_{-\\pi/2}^{\\pi/2}$
Step 6: Evaluate: $a^2[(\\frac{\\pi}{2} + 0) - (-\\frac{\\pi}{2} + 0)] = a^2(\\pi) = \\pi a^2$

📓 Chapter 5 Summary

  • Parametric Equations: x = f(t), y = g(t) describe curves in terms of parameter t
  • dy/dx for Parametrics: $\frac{g'(t)}{f'(t)}$ not quotient rule on second derivative!
  • Parametric Arc Length: $\int \sqrt{(dx/dt)^2 + (dy/dt)^2} \, dt$
  • Polar Coordinates: (r, θ) with x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ
  • Polar Graphs: Circles, roses, limaçons, spirals - understand symmetry
  • Polar Arc Length: $\int \sqrt{r^2 + (dr/dθ)^2} \, dθ$
  • Polar Area: $\frac{1}{2} \int r^2 \, dθ$ - remember the 1/2!
  • Area Between Curves: $\frac{1}{2} \int (r_{outer}^2 - r_{inner}^2) \, dθ$
