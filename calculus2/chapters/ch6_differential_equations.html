Chapter 6: Introduction to Differential Equations

📋 Chapter Overview

Differential equations are equations that involve derivatives. They are the language of change - they describe how things evolve over time. From population growth to radioactive decay, from heat transfer to electrical circuits, differential equations are everywhere in science, engineering, and mathematics.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand what differential equations are and classify them
  • Interpret slope fields and use them to understand behavior
  • Solve separable differential equations
  • Solve linear first-order differential equations
  • Model and analyze population dynamics
  • Solve mixing problems
  • Apply Newton's Law of Cooling

🌍 Real-World Applications

  • Biology: Population growth, spread of diseases
  • Physics: Motion, cooling, radioactive decay
  • Chemistry: Reaction rates, mixing solutions
  • Economics: Compound interest, depreciation
  • Engineering: Circuit analysis, heat transfer

Lesson 39: Introduction to Differential Equations

📖 Introduction

A differential equation is an equation that contains an unknown function and one or more of its derivatives. Solving a differential equation means finding the unknown function that satisfies the equation.

📚 Definition

A differential equation (DE) has the form:

$$F(x, y, y', y'', ..., y^{(n)}) = 0$$

where y is the unknown function of x.

Terminology:

  • Order: The highest derivative that appears (1st order has y', 2nd order has y'', etc.)
  • Solution: A function y = f(x) that satisfies the DE
  • General Solution: Contains arbitrary constants (family of solutions)
  • Particular Solution: Specific values chosen for constants (using initial conditions)
  • Initial Condition: A condition like y(x₀) = y₀ that helps find particular solution

📚 Classification

By Order:

  • First-order: Contains only y', not higher derivatives
  • Second-order: Contains y''
  • n-th order: Contains y^(n)

By Linearity:

  • Linear: y and its derivatives appear linearly (no powers, products, or transcendental functions of y)
  • Nonlinear: y and/or its derivatives appear nonlinearly

Examples:

$y' = 2x$ (first-order, linear)
$y'' + y = 0$ (second-order, linear)
$(y')^2 + y = x$ (first-order, nonlinear - square on y')

🔢 Example 1: Verify that y = e^x is a solution of y' = y

Step 1: Given y = e^x, find y' = e^x (derivative of e^x is e^x)
Step 2: Check the DE: y' should equal y
Step 3: y' = e^x and y = e^x, so y' = y ✓
Step 4: Therefore y = e^x is a solution

Lesson 40: Slope Fields

📖 Introduction

Even before solving a differential equation, we can visualize its solutions using a slope field (or direction field). A slope field shows the slope of solution curves at many points in the plane.

📚 What is a Slope Field?

For a first-order DE y' = f(x, y):

  1. At each point (x, y) in the plane, compute the slope m = f(x, y)
  2. Draw a small line segment with that slope at (x, y)
  3. Do this for many points across the plane

Key Properties:

  • Solution curves are tangent to the slope field at every point
  • The slope field reveals the behavior of all solutions
  • Two different solution curves never cross (if f is continuous)

📚 Interpreting Slope Fields

  • Horizontal segments: y' = 0 at those points
  • Vertical segments: Solution not defined (usually)
  • Patterns: Look for symmetry, periodicity
  • Equilibrium solutions: Where y' = 0 identically (horizontal lines)

🔢 Example: Analyze slope field for y' = x

Step 1: The slope depends only on x, not on y
Step 2: For x = 0: slope is 0 (horizontal lines)
Step 3: For x = 1: slope is 1 (positive diagonal segments)
Step 4: For x = -1: slope is -1 (negative diagonal segments)
Step 5: Slope is same for all y at each x value
Step 6: Solution (general): $\int dy = \int x dx$, so y = \frac{x^2}{2} + C$
Step 7: Family of parabolas - consistent with slope field!

Lesson 41: Separable Differential Equations

📖 Introduction

A separable differential equation is one where we can separate the variables x and y on opposite sides of the equation. These are the easiest type of DE to solve!

📚 Form of Separable Equation

A first-order DE is separable if it can be written as:

$$\\frac{dy}{dx} = g(x)h(y)$$
or equivalently
$$N(y) \\frac{dy}{dx} = M(x)$$

📚 Solution Method

  1. Rewrite as $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{M(x)}{N(y)}$ or similar form
  2. Separate variables: $N(y) \, dy = M(x) \, dx$
  3. Integrate both sides: $\int N(y) \, dy = \int M(x) \, dx$
  4. Solve for y (if possible) to get general solution
  5. Use initial condition to find particular solution

Important Notes:

  • Don't forget the +C on at least one side
  • Careful with absolute values when integrating
  • Check for equilibrium solutions (where h(y) = 0)

🔢 Example 1: Solve y' = x/y with y(0) = 4

Step 1: Rewrite as dy/dx = x/y
Step 2: Separate: y \, dy = x \, dx
Step 3: Integrate: $\int y \, dy = \int x \, dx$
Step 4: Solve: $\frac{y^2}{2} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C$
Step 5: Multiply by 2: $y^2 = x^2 + 2C$
Step 6: Use initial condition y(0)=4: $16 = 0 + 2C$, so $2C = 16$
Step 7: Solution: $y^2 = x^2 + 16$ or $y = \pm \sqrt{x^2 + 16}$
Step 8: Since y(0)=4 > 0, use positive: $y = \sqrt{x^2 + 16}$

Lesson 42: Linear First-Order Differential Equations

📖 Introduction

Linear first-order differential equations have a standard form and a reliable solution method using an "integrating factor." They're not all separable, but we can still solve them!

📚 Standard Form

A linear first-order DE can be written as:

$$\\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)$$

where P(x) and Q(x) are functions of x only.

Key Features:

  • y and its derivative appear linearly (no powers or products)
  • Coefficients depend only on x
  • y' appears to the first power

📚 Solution Method: Integrating Factor

  1. Write in standard form: $\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)$
  2. Find integrating factor: $\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x) \, dx}$
  3. Multiply both sides by $\mu(x)$
  4. Left side becomes $(\mu \cdot y)'$ (verify this!)
  5. Integrate both sides
  6. Solve for y to get general solution

📚 Solution Formula

$$y = e^{-\int P(x) \, dx} \left[ \int e^{\int P(x) \, dx} Q(x) \, dx + C \right]$$

This compact formula contains all the steps - use the process instead of memorizing!

🔢 Example: Solve y' + 2y = 4

Step 1: Already in standard form with P(x) = 2, Q(x) = 4
Step 2: Find integrating factor: $\mu = e^{\int 2 \, dx} = e^{2x}$
Step 3: Multiply by integrating factor: $e^{2x}y' + 2e^{2x}y = 4e^{2x}$
Step 4: Recognize left side: $(e^{2x}y)' = 4e^{2x}$
Step 5: Integrate both sides: $e^{2x}y = \int 4e^{2x} \, dx$
Step 6: Solve: $e^{2x}y = 2e^{2x} + C$
Step 7: Divide by $e^{2x}$: $y = 2 + Ce^{-2x}$

Lesson 43: Applications - Population Models

📖 Introduction

Differential equations naturally model how populations change over time. The simplest models assume the rate of change is proportional to the current population, but more realistic models account for limited resources.

📚 Unrestricted Growth (Malthusian Model)

$$\\frac{dP}{dt} = kP$$

Where: P(t) = population at time t, k = growth constant

Solution (Exponential Growth):

$$P(t) = P_0 e^{kt}$$

where $P_0$ is the initial population.

Interpretation: Population grows without bound (unrealistic in the long term!)

📚 Logistic Growth (Limited Resources)

$$\\frac{dP}{dt} = kP\\left(1 - \\frac{P}{K}\\right)$$

Where:

  • P(t) = population
  • k = intrinsic growth rate
  • K = carrying capacity (maximum sustainable population)

Properties:

  • For P < K: population grows
  • For P > K: population decreases
  • P = K is an equilibrium (carrying capacity)
  • S-curve (sigmoidal) shape

Solution (when P₀ ≠ 0):

$$P(t) = \\frac{K}{1 + Ae^{-kt}}$$

where $A = \\frac{K - P_0}{P_0}$

🔢 Example 1: Bacteria culture starts at 100 and doubles in 3 hours

Step 1: Use exponential growth: P(t) = P₀ e^{kt}
Step 2: Given P₀ = 100, so P(t) = 100e^{kt}
Step 3: Use P(3) = 200: $200 = 100e^{3k}$
Step 4: Solve for k: $2 = e^{3k}$, so $3k = \ln 2$, $k = \frac{\ln 2}{3}$
Step 5: Solution: $P(t) = 100e^{(\\ln 2/3)t} = 100 \\cdot 2^{t/3}$
Step 6: Check: at t=3, P = 100 · 2^1 = 200 ✓

Lesson 44: Applications - Mixing Problems

📖 Introduction

Mixing problems involve tanks being filled and drained, containing a dissolved substance. As solution flows in and out, the concentration changes - leading to a differential equation.

📚 General Setup

For a tank of volume V(t) with x(t) amount of substance:

$$\\frac{dx}{dt} = \\text{(rate in)} - \\text{(rate out)}$$

$\text{rate in} = (\text{flow rate in}) \\times (\text{concentration in})$
$\text{rate out} = (\text{flow rate out}) \\times (\text{concentration out})$

Concentration out: $\frac{x(t)}{V(t)}$ (amount divided by volume)

📚 Key Observations

  • Rate in is usually constant (inlet has fixed concentration)
  • Rate out depends on current concentration
  • Volume may change if inflow ≠ outflow rate
  • DE is often linear (due to rate out term)

🔢 Example: Tank has 100gal with 20lb salt. Fresh water flows in at 3gal/min, mixture out at 3gal/min

Step 1: Let x(t) = amount of salt at time t (in lb)
Step 2: Volume V is constant: 100 gal (inflow = outflow = 3 gal/min)
Step 3: Rate in: 3 × 0 = 0 (fresh water, no salt)
Step 4: Rate out: 3 × (x/100) = 3x/100 = 0.03x
Step 5: DE: dx/dt = 0 - 0.03x = -0.03x
Step 6: This is separable: dx/x = -0.03 dt
Step 7: Integrate: $\ln|x| = -0.03t + C$
Step 8: Solve: $x = Ce^{-0.03t}$ (drop absolute, incorporate into C)
Step 9: Use x(0) = 20: $20 = Ce^0$, so $C = 20$
Step 10: Solution: $x(t) = 20e^{-0.03t}$ lb

Lesson 45: Applications - Cooling & Heating

📖 Introduction

Newton's Law of Cooling states that the rate of change of temperature is proportional to the difference between the object's temperature and the surrounding temperature.

📚 Newton's Law of Cooling/Heating

$$\\frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - T_s)$$

Where:

  • T(t) = temperature of the object at time t
  • T_s = surrounding (ambient) temperature
  • k = constant (negative for cooling, positive for heating)

Alternative Form:

$$\\frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - T_s) \implies T' - kT = -kT_s$$

This is a linear first-order DE we can solve with integrating factor!

📚 Solution Formula

$$T(t) = T_s + (T_0 - T_s)e^{-kt}$$

where T₀ = initial temperature at t = 0

Interpretation:

  • Temperature exponentially approaches T_s
  • The rate depends on temperature difference
  • Can solve for k given temperature at another time

🔢 Example: Baked potato at 200°F in 70°F room. After 20min, temperature is 160°F

Step 1: Newton's Law: dT/dt = k(T - 70)
Step 2: Solution formula: T(t) = 70 + (T₀ - 70)e^{-kt}
Step 3: Given T₀ = 200: T(t) = 70 + 130e^{-kt}
Step 4: Use T(20) = 160: $160 = 70 + 130e^{-20k}$
Step 5: Solve: $90 = 130e^{-20k}$, so $e^{-20k} = 90/130 = 9/13$
Step 6: Take ln: $-20k = \ln(9/13)$, so $k = -\frac{1}{20}\ln(9/13)$
Step 7: Temperature model: $T(t) = 70 + 130e^{-kt}$ where $k = -\frac{1}{20}\ln(9/13)$

📓 Chapter 6 Summary

  • Differential Equations: Equations involving derivatives of unknown functions
  • Classification: By order (highest derivative) and linearity
  • Solution: Function or family of functions satisfying the DE
  • General vs Particular: General has constants, particular satisfies initial conditions
  • Slope Fields: Visualize solutions by showing slopes at grid points
  • Separable DEs: Separate variables - g(y)dy = f(x)dx - then integrate
  • Linear First-Order: y' + P(x)y = Q(x) - use integrating factor $e^{\int P dx}$
  • Population Models: Unrestricted (exponential) and logistic growth
  • Mixing Problems: dx/dt = rate in - rate out
  • Newton's Cooling: dT/dt = k(T - T_s), approach ambient temperature
