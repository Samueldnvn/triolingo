Module Overview

Statics and dynamics form the foundation of mechanical analysis for hexapod robots. Statics deals with bodies at rest and forces in equilibrium, while dynamics analyzes motion under applied forces. Understanding both is essential for design, control, and stability analysis of hexapod robots in various configurations and terrains.

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate forces, moments, and equilibrium conditions
  • Apply Newton's laws to linear and rotational motion
  • Compute center of mass and moment of inertia
  • Analyze static stability for hexapod configurations
  • Apply Newton-Euler equations to robot dynamics

Section 2.1: Forces and Statics

Force:

A force \(\vec{F}\) with magnitude and direction. Measured in Newtons (N). Forces can be contact forces (friction, normal) or body forces (gravity, magnetic).

Resultant Force:
$$\vec{F}_{net} = \sum_i \vec{F}_i$$

The vector sum of all forces acting on a body.

Equilibrium Conditions (Statics)

$$\sum \vec{F} = \vec{0}, \quad \sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{0}$$

For a body in static equilibrium, net force and net torque are zero.

Example: Hexapod in Static Equilibrium

A 60 kg hexapod with 6 legs in contact with ground. Assuming equal weight distribution, each leg supports:

Normal force on each leg: \(N = 60/6 = 10 \text{ N}\) (vertical upward)

Sum of vertical forces: \(6 \times 10 \text{ N up} = 60 \text{ N}\), which balances gravity 60 N down.

Section 2.2: Moments and Torque

Moment of Force (Torque):

The moment of force \(\vec{F}\) about point O, applied at position \(\vec{r}\):

$$\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$$

Magnitude: \(|\vec{\tau}| = rF\sin(\theta)\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec{r}\) and \(\vec{F}\).

Direction: Perpendicular to both \(\vec{r}\) and \(\vec{F}\); follows right-hand rule.

Center of Mass (COM):

For a system of discrete particles or continuous body, COM is the point where the entire mass can be considered concentrated for translational motion. For hexapod robots, the COM shifts as legs move, affecting stability.

Example: Torque on Hexapod Hip

A force of 50 N acts at the end of a 0.3 m lever arm at a \(60^\circ\) angle. The torque is:

$$\tau = rF\sin(\theta) = (0.3 \text{ m})(50 \text{ N})\sin(60^\circ) \approx 13 \text{ Nm}$$

Section 2.3: Dynamics - Newton's Laws

Newton's Second Law (Linear)

$$\sum \vec{F} = m\vec{a}$$

Net force equals mass times acceleration. Also written as: \(\sum \vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt}\), where \(\vec{p} = m\vec{v}\) is momentum.

Newton's Second Law (Rotational)

$$\sum \vec{\tau} = \vec{I}\vec{\alpha}$$

Net torque equals moment of inertia times angular acceleration. The rotational analog of \(\vec{F} = m\vec{a}\).

Newton's Third Law

For every action (force), there is an equal and opposite reaction (force).

$$\vec{F}_{AB} = -\vec{F}_{BA}$$

Section 2.4: Momentum and Inertia

Moment of Inertia (I):

Resistance to rotational acceleration. For discrete objects: \(I = \sum_i m_i r_i^2\). For continuous bodies: \(I = \int r^2 dm\).

Where \(r\) is perpendicular distance from axis of rotation. For discrete particles: \(I = \sum m_i r_i^2\).

Common Moments of Inertia:
  • Thin rod about center: \(I = \frac{1}{12}mL^2\)
  • Thin rod about end: \(I = \frac{1}{3}mL^2\)
  • Solid cylinder (radius R) about central axis: \(I = \frac{1}{2}mR^2\)
  • Hollow cylinder (inner radius \(R_1\), outer \(R_2\)): \(I = \frac{1}{2}m(R_1^2 + R_2^2)\)
  • Solid sphere (radius R): \(I = \frac{2}{5}mR^2\)

Example: Hexapod Leg Moment of Inertia

Leg mass \(m = 0.5 \text{ kg}\), length \(L = 0.4 \text{ m}\). About hip (rotation at one end):

$$I = \frac{1}{3}mL^2 = \frac{1}{3}(0.5)(0.4)^2 = \frac{1}{3}(0.5)(0.16) \approx 0.027 \text{ kg} \cdot \text{m}^2$$
