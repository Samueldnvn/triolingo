Module Overview

Hexapod robotics requires a strong foundation in mathematics including vectors, matrices, calculus, and linear algebra. This module reviews essential concepts needed throughout the course, ensuring you have the mathematical tools to understand mechanics, dynamics, kinematics, and control theory.

Learning Objectives

  • Perform vector operations: dot product, cross product, gradient, divergence, curl
  • Understand matrix operations and transformations
  • Work with multiple coordinate systems and conversions
  • Apply homogeneous transformations for spatial transformations
  • Work with eigenvalues and eigenvectors

Section 1.1: Vector Operations

Dot Product (Scalar Product):

Given two vectors \(\)\vec{A} = [A_x, A_y, A_z]\) and \(\)\vec{B} = [B_x, B_y, B_z]\), the dot product is:

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y + A_z B_z = |\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\cos(\theta)$$

Where \(\)\theta\) is the angle between the vectors. The dot product results in a scalar quantity.

Example: Work Done by a Force

If a hexapod leg exerts force \(\)\vec{F} = [10, 5, 0]\) N through a displacement \(\)\vec{d} = [0.02, 0.03, 0]\) m, the work done is:

$$W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d} = (10)(0.02) + (5)(0.03) + (0)(0) = 0.2 + 0.15 = 0.35 \text{ J}$$
Cross Product (Vector Product):
$$\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = [A_y B_z - A_z B_y, A_z B_x - A_x B_z, A_x B_y - A_y B_x]$$
$$|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = |\vec{A}||\vec{B}|\sin(\theta)$$

Properties of Cross Product

The cross product is not commutative: \(\)\vec{A} \times \vec{B} \neq \vec{B} \times \vec{A}\). In fact, \(\)\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = -(\vec{B} \times \vec{A})\). The direction follows the right-hand rule.

Gradient:

The gradient of a scalar field \(f(x,y,z)\) points in the direction of maximum rate of increase:

$$\nabla f = \left[\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right]$$
Divergence:

The divergence of a vector field \(\)\vec{F} = [F_x, F_y, F_z]\) measures how much the field "spreads out":

$$\nabla \cdot \vec{F} = \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial z}$$
Curl:

The curl of a vector field measures rotation:

$$\nabla \times \vec{F} = \left[\frac{\partial F_z}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial z}, \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial F_y}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial F_x}{\partial y}\right]$$

Vector Identities

  • Divergence of curl: \(\)\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \vec{F}) = 0\) (always)
  • Curl of gradient: \(\)\nabla \times (\nabla f) = 0\) (always true)
  • Laplacian: \(\)\nabla^2 f = \nabla \cdot (\nabla f) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z^2}\)

Section 1.2: Matrix Operations

Matrix Multiplication:

For matrices \(A\) (m×n) and \(B\) (n×p), the product \(C =\) AB\) is:

$$C_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} A_{ik} B_{kj}$$

The result is an m×p matrix. Note that \(AB \)\neq BA\) generally (non-commutative).

Determinant:

The determinant of a matrix determines if it has an inverse.

For 2×2 matrices:

$$\det(A) = \det\begin{bmatrix}a & b\\c & d\end{bmatrix} = ad - bc$$
Matrix Inverse:

A matrix \(A\) has an inverse \(A^{-1\)}\) if and only if \(\)\det(A) \neq 0\). The inverse satisfies \(AA^{-1\)} = A^{-1}A = I\)

Rotation Matrix (z-axis):

Rotation about z-axis by angle \(\)\theta\):

$$R_z(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta & -\sin\theta & 0\\\sin\theta & \cos\theta & 0\\0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

Rotation about x-axis by angle \(\)\theta\):

$$R_x(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 & 0\\0 & \cos\theta & -\sin\theta\\0 & \sin\theta & \cos\theta\end{bmatrix}$$

Rotation about y-axis by angle \(\)\theta\):

$$R_y(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta & 0 & \sin\theta\\0 & 1 & 0\\-\sin\theta & 0 & \cos\theta\end{bmatrix}$$

Rotation matrices are orthogonal: \(R^{-1\)} = R^T\) and \(\)\det(R) = 1\).

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors:

For a square matrix \(A\), eigenvalues \(\)\lambda\) and eigenvectors \(\)\vec{v}\) satisfy:

$$A\vec{v} = \lambda\vec{v}$$

Find eigenvalues by solving \(\)\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\). Eigenvectors reveal principal axes (modes) of transformation.

Section 1.3: Coordinate Systems

Cartesian Coordinates

Cartesian coordinates \((x, y, z)\) are the most common coordinate system, defined by three mutually perpendicular axes meeting at an origin. This system is ideal for:

  • Simple vector addition and subtraction
  • Matrix transformations
  • Robot body frame and world frame

Distance between two points:

$$d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2 + (z_2 - z_1)^2}$$

Spherical Coordinates

A point is specified by \((\rho, \theta, \phi)\) where:

  • \(\)\rho\) = distance from origin
  • \(\)\theta\) = azimuthal angle in xy-plane from x-axis
  • \(\)\phi\) = polar angle from positive z-axis

Conversion to Cartesian:

$$x = \rho \sin\phi \cos\theta, \quad y = \rho \sin\phi \sin\theta, \quad z = \rho \cos\phi$$

Hexapod Leg Mapping

For hexapod control, cylindrical coordinates often map naturally to leg kinematics:

  • Hip azimuth \(\)\leftrightarrow\) \(\)\theta\) (rotation in horizontal plane)
  • Femur elevation \(\)\leftrightarrow\) \(\)\phi\) (angle from vertical)
  • Leg extension \(\)\leftrightarrow\) \(\)\rho\) (distance from hip to foot)

Section 1.4: Homogeneous Transformations

Homogeneous Coordinates:

A point \((x, y, z)\) is represented as \((x, y, z, 1)\). Homogeneous transformations combine rotation and translation in single matrix operation:

$$T = \begin{bmatrix}R & \vec{p}\\0 & 1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}r_{11} & r_{12} & r_{13} & p_x\\r_{21} & r_{22} & r_{23} & p_y\\r_{31} & r_{32} & r_{33} & p_z\\0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}$$

Composition

To apply transformations \(T_1\) then \(T_2\): multiply right to left: \(T =\) T_2 T_1\)
