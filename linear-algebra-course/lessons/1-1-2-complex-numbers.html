Introduction to Complex Numbers

Welcome to the beautiful world of complex numbers! While real numbers cover everything on the number line, complex numbers expand our mathematical universe into two dimensions. They might seem "imaginary" at first, but they're incredibly real and powerful in fields like quantum mechanics, electrical engineering, and signal processing.

In this lesson, we'll explore what complex numbers are, how to work with them, and why they matter. You'll learn about the mysterious imaginary unit $$i$$, complex number operations, and the concept of complex conjugates. Understanding complex numbers opens the door to advanced mathematics and physics!

What Are Complex Numbers?
Definition
Complex Number

A complex number has the form $$z = a + bi$$ where:

  • $$a$$ is called the real part (denoted $$\text{Re}(z)$$)
  • $$b$$ is called the imaginary part (denoted $$\text{Im}(z)$$)
  • $$i$$ is the imaginary unit, where $$i^2 = -1$$ or $$i = \sqrt{-1}$$
Definition
Imaginary Unit

The imaginary unit $$i$$ is defined as the square root of $$-1$$:

Formula
$$i = \sqrt{-1}$$

This means $$i^2 = -1$$, $$i^3 = -i$$, and $$i^4 = 1$$

Example
Identifying Parts of Complex Numbers
Example 1

For $$z = 3 + 4i$$:

Real part $$\text{Re}(z) = 3$$

Imaginary part $$\text{Im}(z) = 4$$

Example 2

For $$z = 5 - 2i$$:

Real part $$\text{Re}(z) = 5$$

Imaginary part $$\text{Im}(z) = -2$$ (negative!)

Example 3

For $$z = -7$$ (which equals $$-7 + 0i$$):

Real part $$\text{Re}(z) = -7$$

Imaginary part $$\text{Im}(z) = 0$$

Example 4

For $$z = 3i$$ (which equals $$0 + 3i$$):

Real part $$\text{Re}(z) = 0$$

Imaginary part $$\text{Im}(z) = 3$$

Complex Number Operations
Operation 1
Addition

Add complex numbers by adding their real parts and imaginary parts separately:

Formula
$$(a + bi) + (c + di) = (a + c) + (b + d)i$$
Operation 2
Subtraction

Subtract complex numbers similarly:

Formula
$$(a + bi) - (c + di) = (a - c) + (b - d)i$$
Operation 3
Multiplication

Multiply like algebraic expressions, remembering that $$i^2 = -1$$:

Formula
$$(a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i$$
Example
Complex Number Calculations
Example 1: Addition

Calculate $$(3 + 2i) + (1 - 4i)$$

Real parts: $$3 + 1 = 4$$

Imaginary parts: $$2 + (-4) = -2$$

Result
$$(3 + 2i) + (1 - 4i) = 4 - 2i$$
Example 2: Multiplication

Calculate $$(2 + 3i)(1 - i)$$

Method 1: Expand and simplify:

$$(2 + 3i)(1 - i) = 2(1) + 2(-i) + 3i(1) + 3i(-i)$$

$$= 2 - 2i + 3i - 3i^2$$

$$= 2 + i - 3(-1)$$ (since $$i^2 = -1$$)

$$= 2 + i + 3 = 5 + i$$

Result
$$(2 + 3i)(1 - i) = 5 + i$$
Example 3: Powers of i

Simplify $$i^{13}$$

Since $$i^4 = 1$$, we find: $$13 = 4 \times 3 + 1$$

$$i^{13} = i^{4 \times 3 + 1} = (i^4)^3 \cdot i^1 = 1^3 \cdot i = i$$

Result
$$i^{13} = i$$
Complex Conjugates
Definition
Complex Conjugate

The complex conjugate of $$z = a + bi$$, denoted by $$\overline{z}$$, is:

Formula
$$\overline{z} = a - bi$$

You simply change the sign of the imaginary part!

Example
Finding Complex Conjugates
Example 1

Find $$\overline{3 + 4i}$$

$$\overline{3 + 4i} = 3 - 4i$$

Example 2

Find $$\overline{5 - 2i}$$

$$\overline{5 - 2i} = 5 + 2i$$

Example 3

Show that a number times its conjugate is always real

$$z \cdot \overline{z} = (a + bi)(a - bi) = a^2 - abi + abi - b^2i^2$$

$$= a^2 - b^2(-1) = a^2 + b^2$$

The product is always real!

Important
Key Properties of Complex Conjugates
  • $$\overline{\overline{z}} = z$$ - The conjugate of a conjugate gives you back the original
  • $$\overline{z + w} = \overline{z} + \overline{w}$$ - Conjugate of sum equals sum of conjugates
  • $$\overline{z \cdot w} = \overline{z} \cdot \overline{w}$$ - Conjugate of product equals product of conjugates
  • $$z \cdot \overline{z} = |z|^2$$ - Product equals squared magnitude (always real)
Magnitude of Complex Numbers
Definition
Magnitude (Modulus)

The magnitude (or modulus) of a complex number $$z = a + bi$$, denoted by $$|z|$$, is:

Formula
$$|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$$

This represents the distance from the origin to the point $$(a, b)$$ in the complex plane.

Example
Calculating Magnitudes
Example 1

Find $$|3 + 4i|$$

$$|3 + 4i| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$

The magnitude is 5.

Example 2

Find $$| -5 + 12i|$$

$$| -5 + 12i| = \sqrt{(-5)^2 + 12^2} = \sqrt{25 + 144} = \sqrt{169} = 13$$

The magnitude is 13.

Example 3

Show that $$|3 - 4i| = |3 + 4i|$$

$$|3 - 4i| = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$

$$|3 + 4i| = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$$

Conjugates have the same magnitude!

Fact

The complex plane is a 2D plane where the x-axis represents the real part and the y-axis represents the imaginary part. Each complex number corresponds to a point $$(a, b)$$ on this plane.

Real Numbers as Subset of Complex Numbers
Connection
Real Numbers Are Complex Too!

Every real number can be written as a complex number with imaginary part zero. For example:

  • $$7 = 7 + 0i$$
  • $$-3 = -3 + 0i$$
  • $$0 = 0 + 0i$$

This means the real numbers $$\mathbb{R}$$ are a subset of the complex numbers $$\mathbb{C}$$.

Warning
Not All Complex Numbers Are Real

However, many complex numbers like $$3 + 4i$$ or $$5i$$ are NOT real numbers. They truly live in the complex number system!

Why Complex Numbers Matter
Applications
Real-World Uses

Complex numbers are essential in many fields:

  • Quantum Mechanics: Wave functions are complex-valued
  • Electrical Engineering: AC circuit analysis uses complex numbers
  • Signal Processing: Fourier transforms use complex exponentials
  • Control Theory: System stability analysis
  • Fluid Dynamics: Potential flow theory
  • Fractals: The famous Mandelbrot set is defined using complex numbers

Summary
Summary

In this lesson, we covered:

  • Definition of complex numbers: $$z = a + bi$$
  • The imaginary unit $$i$$ where $$i^2 = -1$$
  • Complex number operations: addition, subtraction, multiplication
  • Complex conjugates and their properties
  • Magnitude (modulus) of complex numbers
  • Real numbers as a subset of complex numbers
  • Applications in physics and engineering

Complex numbers extend mathematics beautifully into two dimensions. They're not just theoretical - they're practical tools that power modern science and technology!
