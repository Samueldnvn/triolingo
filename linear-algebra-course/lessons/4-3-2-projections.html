Orthogonal Projection

The orthogonal projection of vector v onto vector w (where w is nonzero) is the component of v in the direction of w. It represents the "shadow" of v on w.

Projection Formula

The projection of v onto w is given by:

proj_w(v) = \frac{v \cdot w}{w \cdot w} w = \frac{v \cdot w}{|w|^2} w

Where v dot w is the dot product and |w| is the magnitude of w.

Key Insight

The factor (v dot w) / (w dot w) is a scalar that determines how far along w the projection lands.

Example 1: Basic Projection

Find the projection of v = [3, 4] onto w = [1, 0].

Step 1: Compute v dot w

v \cdot w = 3(1) + 4(0) = 3

Step 2: Compute w dot w

w \cdot w = 1(1) + 0(0) = 1

Step 3: Apply the formula

proj_w(v) = \frac{3}{1} [1, 0] = [3, 0]

Interpretation: The projection of [3, 4] onto the x-axis is [3, 0], which makes geometric sense.

Example 2: Non-Axis Projection

Find the projection of v = [2, 3] onto w = [1, 2].

Step 1: Compute v dot w and w dot w

v \cdot w = 2(1) + 3(2) = 2 + 6 = 8
w \cdot w = 1(1) + 2(2) = 1 + 4 = 5

Step 2: Apply the formula

proj_w(v) = \frac{8}{5} [1, 2] = \left[\frac{8}{5}, \frac{16}{5}\right]

Decimal form: [1.6, 3.2]

Projection onto a Subspace

To project a vector v onto a subspace W of R^n given by basis vectors {w1, w2, ..., wk}, we use the projection matrix formula:

P_W = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T

Where A is the matrix whose columns are the basis vectors of W.

Important Note

This formula assumes the basis vectors are linearly independent (forming a basis for W).

Projection onto an Orthonormal Basis

When the basis {u1, u2, ..., uk} is orthonormal, the formula simplifies:

proj_W(v) = (v \cdot u_1)u_1 + (v \cdot u_2)u_2 + ... + (v \cdot u_k)u_k

This is much easier to compute and is why orthonormal bases are preferred.

Orthogonal Component

The component of v orthogonal to w is:

v_\perp = v - proj_w(v)

This satisfies v = proj_w(v) + v_\perp, where proj_w(v) is parallel to w and v_\perp is orthogonal to w.

Pythagorean Theorem for Vectors

The magnitude of v can be expressed as:

|v|^2 = |proj_w(v)|^2 + |v_\perp|^2
Example 3: Finding Both Components

For v = [5, 2] and w = [1, 1], find proj_w(v) and the orthogonal component.

Step 1: Compute dot products

v \cdot w = 5(1) + 2(1) = 7
w \cdot w = 1(1) + 1(1) = 2

Step 2: Find projection

proj_w(v) = \frac{7}{2} [1, 1] = \left[\frac{7}{2}, \frac{7}{2}\right]

Step 3: Find orthogonal component

v_\perp = v - proj_w(v) = [5, 2] - \left[\frac{7}{2}, \frac{7}{2}\right] = \left[\frac{3}{2}, -\frac{3}{2}\right]

Verification: Check that v_\perp dot w = 0:

\left[\frac{3}{2}, -\frac{3}{2}\right] \cdot [1, 1] = \frac{3}{2} - \frac{3}{2} = 0

The orthogonal component is indeed orthogonal to w!

Properties of Projections
  • proj_w(v) is always a scalar multiple of w
  • proj_w(proj_w(v)) = proj_w(v) (projection is idempotent)
  • The projection matrix P is symmetric: P^T = P
  • The projection matrix is idempotent: P^2 = P
  • proj_w(c v) = c proj_w(v) for any scalar c
  • proj_w(v1 + v2) = proj_w(v1) + proj_w(v2)
